What is a gas?

Reading · States of matter
All matter is made of tiny particles (atoms and molecules) that are constantly moving. The way those particles are arranged and how fast they move determines whether something is a solid, liquid, or gas.

In a solid, particles are packed tightly in a fixed pattern. They vibrate but don't move from their place — that's why solids have a definite shape and volume.

In a liquid, particles are still close together but can slide past each other. Liquids have a fixed volume but take the shape of their container.

In a gas, particles move very fast and are far apart. They have no fixed shape and no fixed volume — they spread out to completely fill whatever container they are in. Because of all that empty space between particles, gases can be compressed (squeezed) into a smaller volume.

Reading · Gas particles and pressure
Gas particles don't just float — they collide constantly with each other and with the walls of their container. Every collision pushes on the wall a tiny bit. The sum of all those tiny pushes creates what we call pressure.

Think of a basketball: the air inside is hitting the rubber walls millions of times per second. All those tiny hits together create the pressure that keeps the ball inflated.

Two things increase pressure:
• More particles in the same space (more hits per second)
• Smaller space for the same number of particles (particles hit walls more often because they don't have to travel as far)

Boyle's Law: Pressure & Volume

BOYLE'S LAW · ROBERT BOYLE, 1662
P × V = constant (k)
If the temperature stays the same, pressure and volume have an inverse relationship: when one goes up, the other goes down.
📌 Squeeze the gas → volume ↓ → pressure ↑
📌 Release the gas → volume ↑ → pressure ↓
Reading · Why does this happen?
Imagine you have 20 gas particles inside a box. The particles bounce around and hit the walls a certain number of times per second — let's say 100 hits per second total. That's your pressure.

Now you squeeze the box to half its original size. You still have 20 particles, but now they travel a shorter distance before hitting a wall. They hit the walls twice as often — now 200 hits per second. Pressure doubled.

This is Boyle's Law in action: halve the volume → double the pressure. The math works perfectly: P × V always gives the same number (as long as temperature doesn't change).

Charles's Law: Volume & Temperature

CHARLES'S LAW · JACQUES CHARLES, 1787
V / T = constant (k)
If pressure stays the same, volume and temperature have a direct relationship: when one goes up, the other goes up too.
📌 Heat the gas → particles move faster → gas expands → volume ↑
📌 Cool the gas → particles slow down → gas shrinks → volume ↓
Reading · Temperature and particle speed
Temperature is a measure of how fast particles are moving. Hot = fast particles. Cold = slow particles.

When you heat a gas, particles gain energy and move faster. They hit the walls harder and more often. If the container can expand (like a balloon), it grows to keep the pressure the same. If the container is rigid (like a sealed metal tank), the pressure rises instead.

In Charles's Law, we assume the container can expand (pressure stays constant). So heating → expansion, cooling → contraction.

Important: temperature must be in Kelvin (K) for the math to work.
Formula to convert: K = °C + 273

Gases in technology & daily life

Reading · 5 applications
Gas laws aren't just theory — engineers use them constantly to design technology that helps people. Here are five examples. Read each one carefully; you will use them in a partner task below.
💉

Medical syringes

Pulling the plunger back increases the volume inside the syringe, which drops the pressure. The higher outside pressure then pushes the liquid in. Boyle's Law.

🚲

Bicycle tires on a hot day

When the temperature rises, the air inside a tire expands. Since the tire can't stretch much, pressure increases. Dangerously over-inflated tires on hot days: Charles's Law.

🔥

Fire extinguishers

CO₂ is compressed at very high pressure into a small tank (Boyle's Law). When the valve opens, the gas expands rapidly, cooling and smothering fires.

🎈

Hot air balloons

Heating the air inside the balloon makes it expand (Charles's Law). The expanded hot air is less dense than the cooler air outside, so the balloon rises.

♨️

Steam power plants (next unit!)

Water is heated to create steam — a gas at high temperature and pressure. The expanding steam pushes turbine blades to generate electricity. Both Boyle's and Charles's Laws apply.
