Section 01

Matter & Its Properties

Matter is anything that has mass and occupies space. It exists in different states depending on how its particles are arranged and how much energy they have. Understanding matter's properties helps us predict and control how it behaves.

🧊

Solid

Particles are tightly packed in a fixed arrangement. Definite shape and volume. Low kinetic energy. Examples: iron, ice, rock salt.

💧

Liquid

Particles are close but can move past each other. Definite volume, no fixed shape. Examples: water, mercury, ethanol.

💨

Gas

Particles move freely with high kinetic energy. No definite shape or volume; expands to fill its container. Examples: oxygen, nitrogen, steam.

⚡

Plasma

Ionized gas with extremely high energy. Most common state in the universe. Examples: lightning, stars, neon signs.

Physical vs. Chemical Properties

Property Type Definition Examples Measured without…
Physical Can be observed or measured without changing the substance's identity. Color, density, melting point, boiling point, hardness, conductivity …changing the substance
Chemical Describes how a substance reacts with other substances or changes into a different substance. Flammability, reactivity with acid, tendency to rust, toxicity …observing the reaction itself
💡

Think About It

You can cut a piece of gold into smaller pieces (physical change — still gold). But if you dissolve zinc in acid, it becomes a completely different substance (chemical change). Which property — physical or chemical — lets you predict each scenario?

Physical Changes

A physical change alters the form or appearance of matter but does not change its chemical composition. The substance remains the same substance — no new molecules are formed.

Characteristics of Physical Changes

How to recognize them in the real world

  • The chemical formula of the substance stays the same (e.g., H₂O remains H₂O whether ice, liquid, or steam).
  • The change is usually reversible — you can get the original substance back.
  • No new substances are formed; only the size, shape, or state changes.
  • Energy (heat, pressure, light) drives the change but does not create new bonds.

Section 03

Chemical Changes

A chemical change (chemical reaction) produces one or more new substances with different properties and a different chemical composition. Energy is absorbed or released as chemical bonds are broken and new ones are formed.

⚙️ Physical Change

Same substance, different form

  • No new substances formed
  • Usually reversible
  • Chemical formula unchanged
  • Energy changes state only
Melting wax Tearing fabric Freezing water

🔥 Chemical Change

New substances with new properties

  • New substances (products) formed
  • Usually irreversible
  • Chemical formula changes
  • Energy absorbed or released
Burning wood Rusting iron Cooking an egg

🔍 6 Signs of a Chemical Change

🔥

Heat or Light Produced

Exothermic reactions release energy. Example: combustion of methane produces heat and light (flame).

🌈

Color Change

A new color indicates a new substance. Example: iron turns reddish-brown when it rusts (forms iron oxide).

💭

Gas Produced

Bubbles forming in a solution may indicate a new gas. Example: vinegar + baking soda → CO₂ gas.

🌧️

Precipitate Forms

A solid forms from two liquids mixing. Example: silver nitrate + sodium chloride → white AgCl precipitate.

👃

Odor Change

A new or different smell indicates a new substance. Example: food spoiling produces new compounds with distinct odors.

🌡️

Temperature Change

Endothermic reactions absorb energy — surroundings feel cold. Example: cold packs use endothermic reactions.

⚠️

Important Distinction

These signs suggest a chemical change but don't always confirm it. Temperature change can be physical (dissolving salts). Always look for evidence of a new substance being formed to confirm a chemical reaction occurred.

Section 04

Technology Applications

The interactions between matter and energy are not just theoretical — they power the technologies we rely on every day. Engineers design systems that deliberately harness physical and chemical changes to produce useful outputs.

🔋

Batteries & Fuel Cells

Chemical → Electrical energy. Electrochemical reactions in batteries convert stored chemical energy into electric current. Lithium-ion batteries power phones, laptops, and EVs.

🚗

Internal Combustion Engine

Chemical → Mechanical energy. Combustion of fuel (a chemical change) releases energy that moves pistons, converting thermal energy to motion.

☀️

Solar Cells (Photovoltaics)

Light → Electrical energy. Photons strike a semiconductor (silicon), exciting electrons — a physical quantum interaction that generates electricity with no moving parts.

🏥

Medical Imaging & Drugs

Chemical reactions in medicine. MRI uses nuclear spin interactions (physical). Drugs work through targeted chemical reactions in the body (enzyme inhibitors, receptor binding).

🏗️

Steel & Alloy Production

Physical + chemical changes. Iron ore undergoes reduction (chemical) and then shaping through rolling and forging (physical) to produce structural steel.

🌿

Biofuels & Fermentation

Biochemical → Fuel energy. Microorganisms convert sugars through fermentation (a chemical change) into ethanol, which can replace or supplement fossil fuels.

Summary

Key Takeaways

1️⃣

Matter has properties

Physical properties can be observed without changing the substance. Chemical properties describe how matter reacts and transforms.

2️⃣

Physical changes alter form

The substance's identity stays the same. State changes, cutting, and dissolving are physical changes. Usually reversible.

3️⃣

Chemical changes make new substances

New bonds form, new substances appear. Look for heat/light, gas, color change, precipitate, or odor as signs.

4️⃣

Technology harnesses both

Engineers design systems that convert energy through controlled physical and chemical changes — from batteries to engines to solar cells.
